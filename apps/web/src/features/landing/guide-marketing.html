There are 8 billion people on Earth and almost none of them know your company exists. That's the real problem. Not the product, not the code. If you're reading this, you almost certainly have a top-of-funnel problem, and this is the map for fixing it.

The old marketing principle is the "Rule of Seven": people need to see your brand seven or more times before they trust you enough to buy. Your whole job is to engineer those exposures, cheaply and repeatedly, in the places your customer already is.

One warning before anything else. If you're early (pre-seed to Series A), pick one to three channels, not all of them. Startups reach scale by doubling and tripling down on one primary growth engine. Try to do everything and you do a lot of nothing.

Building is the easy part now. Anyone can ship a product in a weekend. Distribution is the moat, and almost nobody gets good at it because it's a grind.

II

Where your audience lives

Every platform rewards a different kind of content and reaches a different person. Match the channel to who actually buys from you, not to where you personally like to scroll.

Platform Who's there What performs
X Huge startup and tech crowd. The most extreme, opinionated version of you. Long-form takes.
LinkedIn College-educated, reads long text. Short written stories with an image. Video underperforms.
YouTube High-intent, sitting at a computer to learn. Tutorials and depth. Viewers are in a buying-research mindset.
Instagram Higher income, highest content-quality bar. UGC, skits, memes. Self-help and hobby niches.
Facebook 30+ audience, less saturated. Lower quality bar. Images, comparisons, promotions.
TikTok 14 to 28, lowest spending power. Raw talking-head, scrappy edits. Not polished ads.
Reddit Niche communities for literally everything. Genuine value, never a pitch. Your first users hide here.

Every channel eventually saturates. The game is to find the pocket of cheap attention (the alpha) and exploit it before everyone else piles in and drives the cost up.

III

Organic first, paid later

Your marketing mix should evolve as you grow. Almost every startup gets the order wrong and lights money on fire running ads before they've earned the right to.

Start 100% organic

  • Prove people actually want what you're building.
  • Get to know your customer deeply, in their own words.
  • Generate your first revenue.
  • Build the content-creation muscle you'll need later.

Shift to paid only after PMF

  • Once you have revenue, you can finally calculate what a customer is worth.
  • Paid gives you targeted, predictable reach that organic can't control.
  • Start small, around $200 to $300 a day, then scale what works.
  • If organic users don't convert, paid traffic won't either.

The organic engine (finding where your people are and showing up there consistently) is exactly what Sentrive automates. It works out your ICP, finds the communities that fit, and runs the distribution so you can build the content muscle without the grind eating your week.

IV

Founder-led and the viral writing framework

Humans are emotional buyers. They buy from people. Founder-led content is the cheapest, highest-trust channel you have, and the biggest misconception about it is that virality is decided by the algorithm gods. It isn't. Great content performs regardless of posting time or hashtags.

Write for one person, not yourself

The most common beginner mistake is treating content like a personal diary, overusing "I" instead of speaking to "you." Pick an ideal viewer (the easiest one is your own younger self) and dig into their real pain points and aspirations. Then earn the right to talk by leading with a credential or a genuine lived experience.

The framework that carries most posts

The advice / story structure
  1. Hook. One line. If it's weak, everything after it dies unread.
  2. Credibility. One sentence proving you actually lived this problem.
  3. Relatable problem. A pain the reader feels right now.
  4. Context. "Before I get into how to actually do X, you need this."
  5. Unobvious answer. No generic platitudes. Surprise them.
  6. Explanation. 2 to 3 sentences, because it doesn't land on first read.
  7. Specific example. 3 sentences painting a concrete picture.
  8. Takeaway. The whole thing in one line.
  9. CTA. An invitation to follow or comment, never a command.

The hook is where you win or lose

Keep written posts under about 1,800 characters so the whole thing fits one mobile screen. The first three sentences should be short, punchy, and jargon-free. A great trick is giving the punchline up front.

Hook example"We charged $100 a month for an AI that was really just two guys surviving on pizza."

That single line opens a story a $1B startup actually told, and the post it anchored pulled millions of impressions and landed major press with zero ad spend. It works because it's specific, human, and a little absurd.

Manufacture virality with the cover-song method

You don't have to invent format from scratch. Find a post in your niche that already went viral (1,000+ likes, ideally more), then do your own better version of that proven hook and structure. The topic and shape are already market-validated, you're just covering the song. Some proven hook shapes:

  • Rejection. "We got rejected three times. Today we're one of the most-used products in our space."
  • Anniversary. "Five years ago today, I posted my first video."
  • Controversial take. "You should probably be skipping most of your meetings."
The AI tell

Em dashes are a dead giveaway that AI wrote it, and a lot of readers now have AI blindness. The moment a post smells machine-generated, even a good one gets skipped. Write it like a human, read the hook out loud three times before posting, and cut anything that sounds like a robot.

V

Organic short-form, the volume game

Short-form video is a volume and experimentation game, not a talent lottery. On average it takes a creator up to 100 videos to land their first million-view hit. With a real framework, many hit it inside 10 to 30. The mindset is the same either way: you gain clarity by posting, not by thinking.

The trajectory nobody warns you about

  • The flatline (months 1 to 2). Barely any views. This is the learning phase where you master lighting, editing, and camera confidence.
  • The plateau. Where most people quit, right before it works.
  • The click. Stay consistent and the fundamentals become second nature, then growth spikes exponentially instead of linearly.

Rules that compound

  • Don't delete underperformers unless they cause real backlash. Algorithms prefer accounts that don't yank videos, and every flop is data (watch time, retention, follower conversion).
  • Use trial reels and burner accounts to test concepts freely. Anything that clears 10k views is a signal worth expanding.
  • Change at least one element every video. Post and pray is not a strategy.
VI

UGC at scale

User-generated content means contracting anywhere from 5 to 100 everyday people to make copycat versions of the viral content your customer already watches. It looks more authentic than an ad and it's cheap per unit, but it's operationally complex.

Should you even run it?

Run UGC if your customer is on social daily, already consuming content about your space, your product has a simple showable workflow, and most of your revenue is B2C. The creators don't need to be your customer, they just need to accurately recreate the content your customer consumes.

The numbers that make it real

$20-30
cost per in-house video
3-6 mo
painful learning period before the flywheel spins
<$1
CPM is excellent (industry standard is $3 to $5)

One voice-dictation startup went from 0 to 500M views in three months this way, with roughly 40% of app downloads coming from it, run by a 19-year-old with no prior experience. The counterintuitive lesson from programs like it: hire inexperienced creators and give them creative freedom. Around 70 to 80% of top performers had never made content before, because experienced creators are biased by what worked for other audiences.

How to actually stand it up

  • Prefer in-house over an agency. You develop real viral sense about your own product and can turn winners into paid ads later.
  • Use a coach, not a doer. A coach trains everyday people to create and approves every video before it posts, keeping a high bar so money isn't wasted on clips with zero chance of going viral.
  • Start with about 5 creators, not 40. The first two months are chaos while you build the pipeline. Once you find your viral formats, you can onboard a new creator in a week.
  • Send inconsistent creators 2 to 4 specific scripts to remove decision paralysis. Most inconsistency is "I don't know what to film," not laziness.
VII

Faceless branding

A faceless brand grows without a single person on camera. It removes the founder as a bottleneck, turns content into a standalone asset, and can go hyper-viral because cold viewers engage immediately with no parasocial buy-in required.

The goal is digital real estate

Think of every niche as land. Your job is to own a specific plot so completely that you become the account people associate with that topic. You do that by being an engagement leader (high comments and shares relative to views) inside a niche narrow enough to actually dominate.

Backward-map to a sub-sub-niche
  1. Product: cooking with alternative ingredients
  2. Broad niche: cooking (too saturated to win)
  3. Sub-niche: unique cooking strategies
  4. Sub-sub-niche: cooking with artisanal plant proteins (now you can be the authority)

When your free content is this good and this specific, buying the product becomes the only logical next step. The viewer thinks "if the free stuff is this good, the product must be incredible."

Forced creativity beats waiting for inspiration

You don't need to be naturally creative, you need to be a disciplined analyst. Find outlier videos (5M+ views) in niches unrelated to yours, break down the hook frame by frame (visual, audio, on-screen text, cut timing), then merge two proven elements that have never been combined before. A visual hook from a fitness video plus a story structure from a finance video makes something new the algorithm loves.

Engineer the hook and retention

  • Open a loop. Ask a question up front and withhold the answer until the end. The viewer feels a need to close the loop, which maximizes watch time.
  • Use And, But, Therefore. Context (and), conflict (but), payoff (therefore). It keeps constant forward momentum so nobody swipes away mid-video.
  • Reply to every comment in the first hour, and if it's empty, post the first icebreaker yourself to lower the barrier for everyone else.
VIII

Paid ads, the money engine

The whole game in one sentence: acquire a customer for less than they generate in revenue. Simple to say, brutal to execute, and it exposes bad economics instantly.

Know these numbers or don't start

3x+
LTV to CAC ratio for healthy economics
$30-60k
typical loss during the learning phase
3 mo
before the algorithm truly understands your customer

If your CAC exceeds your LTV, scaling just burns money faster. You cannot calculate LTV without revenue, which is the whole reason paid ads come after product-market fit, never before.

The order of operations

  1. Set up attribution before you spend a dollar. Without it you're handing money to the ad platform blind. Use the right tracking stack for web or mobile and make sure a real conversion event (a purchase, a deposit, a paid subscription) is firing, not a soft signal like a signup.
  2. Know your numbers. Average revenue per user, revenue per conversion event, and how long until a user starts paying you back.
  3. Pick 1 to 2 platforms, not five. TikTok trains fastest (1.5 to 2 weeks) and is a good learning ground. Meta trains slower (1 to 2 months) but targets better and scales further. Google is highest intent. Splitting budget across many platforms slows learning everywhere.
  4. Test creative systematically. 3 to 4 new ads a day when starting. Spend $300 to $400 per creative, run each for 3 weeks minimum, kill losers, graduate winners.

Two rules people always break

  • Tell the platform to find buyers, not viewers. Running an awareness campaign when you want sales trains the algorithm on the wrong goal. A video with 10k views and 200 conversions beats one with 1M views and 100 conversions every time.
  • The 100x rule. Spend about 100 times your target cost per acquisition on a creative to give the algorithm enough data to learn. Underspend and optimization is impossible.

80% of ad performance is the creative itself. And most founders quit in weeks 4 to 8, right before the algorithm starts working. Budget for the pain up front or don't begin.

IX

Influencer marketing

A high-variance channel. Done well it delivers outsized reach, credibility, and creative leverage. Done poorly it's a fast way to burn cash. It works best under specific conditions, so be honest about whether you meet them.

When it actually works

  • You need to borrow trust fast in a regulated or trust-sensitive niche (finance, health, Web3).
  • Your audience expects high-production content that scrappy UGC can't deliver.
  • Your product has a simple, novel workflow that allows a "soft sell," where it appears naturally inside the creator's normal content so viewers feel they discovered it.
  • You have enough capital to absorb the inevitable early failures and the high CPMs.

How the pros run it

  • Selection. Prioritize creators with consistent view counts (100k+), a mostly US audience, who speak on camera. Be wary of anyone represented by an agency or saturated with big-brand deals.
  • Sourcing. Native TikTok and Instagram algorithms beat hashtag tools. Engage heavily with example creators so the app surfaces similar ones, then reach out to book a call.
  • Negotiation. Ask their rate first, anchor to a $2 to $3 CPM on average views, and structure deals around minimum view commitments with bonuses for hitting big numbers.
  • The winning move. Once a creator's post goes viral organically, buy the rights and run paid spend behind it. That amplification of proven creative is where the real scale comes from.

One skills-based gaming app scaled to nine figures of revenue partly this way: one video asset from a big name, then heavy paid spend behind it. But views rarely translate to conversions for complex products, so it lives and dies on execution.

X

GEO and the AI-search frontier

Generative Engine Optimization is getting your brand cited inside AI answers (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews) the way SEO got you ranked on Google. It's the newest channel and not yet mature, so treat it as a frontier bet, mostly worth it if you're well-funded and want to be early.

The useful news is that what works for SEO largely works for GEO. Clean structure, real authority, and content that answers actual questions. A site can hold the number one Google ranking and still be invisible to every AI crawler, so the opportunity is in being one of the few that shows up when someone asks an AI for a recommendation in your category.

Reddit is quietly one of the biggest inputs into AI answers, since models lean heavily on it. That's another reason the organic community game compounds: showing up genuinely across the right subreddits doesn't just get you users today, it feeds how AI describes your category tomorrow. XI

The team and the principles

If you're building a growth team from scratch at Series A and beyond, keep it small so you iterate fast. A lean, effective setup is roughly five people: a performance marketer for paid, a young creator with viral sense, a video editor producing 4 to 5 ads a day, a UGC manager or coach, and one organizer to wrangle the creative chaos. Fewer decision-makers means faster iteration and less analysis paralysis.

Everything in this playbook comes back to a handful of principles:

  • Pick one channel and master it before adding another. Splitting focus slows learning everywhere.
  • Ship the embarrassing version. Get the worst thing that works in front of real people and iterate on feedback. Don't build for months before you know anyone wants it.
  • Let data pick winners, not your ego. Your opinion on what "should" work doesn't matter. Only what converts does.
  • Proof beats claims. Lead every post, pitch, and ad with a real number. Receipts, not promises.
  • Volume plus consistency plus continuous improvement. Every channel here is a marathon that pays off right around the point most people quit.
Find the alpha, exploit it before it saturates, and go deep on one thing instead of dabbling in ten.
